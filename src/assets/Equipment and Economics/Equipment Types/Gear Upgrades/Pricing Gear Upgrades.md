This section is mostly for people who are interested in making up new items, or are curious how I decided pricing.

Gear prices are generally calculated by what level the creature is expected to obtain them. I took what I considered the average profession bonus of the a character at a given level and then used that to find the value of 4 weeks of work on the [[Economics]] page.

I consider an average profession bonus to be 5+1.33([[LvL]]). This estimation assumes that someone will have racial skill bonuses, [[Skill Focus]] and other etc additions from class/items/circumstance to eventually increase the skill, hence the 1.3x multiplier instead of 5+LvL. Using this formula, we can make a simple chart.

| Level | Skill Bonus | Value Generation (4 weeks, approximated) |
| ----- | ----------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| 1     | 6           | 2,300                                    |
| 2     | 7           | 2,800                                    |
| 3     | 9           | 4,000                                    |
| 4     | 10          | 4,800                                    |
| 5     | 11          | 5,700                                    |
| 6     | 13          | 7,600                                    |
| 7     | 14          | 8,700                                    |
| 8     | 15          | 9,800                                    |
| 9     | 17          | 12,400                                   |
| 10    | 18          | 13,800                                   |
| 11    | 19          | 15,300                                   |
| 12    | 21          | 18,500                                   |
Now obviously we cant have every item cost this value. Legendary weapons crafted by level 1 guys in few months? Nah. We can take these values and multiply them depending on the rarity, power etc. This will create some overlap, as super powerful items for a level 2 character probably aren't that big a deal for a level 10 character.

Multipliers can be 1.5x, 2x, 4x or even 10x, 50x for incredibly powerful or rare items. Cheaper items tend to be things that have limited uses each day, are relatively mundane or have a specific niche.
